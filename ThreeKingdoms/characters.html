Cao Wei

Cao Cao

Name: Cao Cao (曹操, courtesy name Mengde)

Years: 155–220 AD

Role: Warlord and Chancellor of Wei

Bio: Consolidated northern China under his control; renowned strategist and poet.

Famous for his decisive victory at the Battle of Guandu (200 AD), which cemented his power against Yuan Shao.

Cao Pi

Name: Cao Pi (曹丕, courtesy name Zihuan)

Years: 187–226 AD

Role: First Emperor of Wei

Bio: Son of Cao Cao, Cao Pi formally ended the Han dynasty and established the state of Wei in 220 AD.

Known for consolidating power, promoting literature, and centralizing government administration.

Cao Rui

Name: Cao Rui (曹叡, courtesy name Yuanzhi)

Years: 205–239 AD

Role: Second Emperor of Wei

Bio: Grandson of Cao Cao, he succeeded Cao Pi in 226 AD.

Cao Rui strengthened the empire’s administration, oversaw construction projects, and defended Wei against Shu and Wu during the Three Kingdoms conflicts.

Cao Fang

Name: Cao Fang (曹芳, courtesy name Dao)

Years: 231–274 AD

Role: Third Emperor of Wei

Bio: Adopted grandson of Cao Rui, became emperor at a young age.

His reign was dominated by regents and court officials, limiting his personal influence. His era saw continued military struggles with Shu and Wu.

Cao Mao

Name: Cao Mao (曹髦, courtesy name Shining)

Years: 241–260 AD

Role: Fourth Emperor of Wei

Bio: Became emperor at age 13. Attempted to assert his authority over powerful regents but was ultimately killed in a failed coup.

Remembered as a young ruler who tried to reclaim imperial power.

Cai Wenji

Name: Cai Yan (蔡琰, courtesy name Cai Wenji)

Years: c. 170–c. 220 AD

Role: Poet and Musician

Bio: Renowned for her poetry and music; captured by Xiongnu (roughly modern Mongolia and northern China), later returned to Han China.

Cao Hong

Name: Cao Hong (曹洪, courtesy name Zilian)

Years: ?–232 AD

Role: General and cousin of Cao Cao

Bio: Saved Cao Cao’s life during an early campaign; later served in many of Wei’s major battles.

His loyalty was sometimes questioned but ultimately recognized by the court.

Cao Ren

Name: Cao Ren (曹仁, courtesy name Zixiao)

Bio: Served as a trusted general under Cao Cao; known for his strategic acumen and loyalty.

Role: General of Wei

Bio: Son of Cao Ren and a leading commander under Cao Pi and Cao Rui. Led multiple campaigns against Shu, continuing Wei’s efforts to dominate the west.

Bio: Early supporter of Cao Cao and one of his key advisers; known for his pragmatic, sometimes ruthless counsel. Played a major role in the victory at Guandu.

Shu Han

Liu Bei

Name: Liu Bei (劉備, courtesy name Xuande)

Years: 161–223 CE

Role: Founder of Shu Han

Bio: Known for benevolence and leadership, Liu Bei established Shu Han during the Three Kingdoms period. He valued loyalty and cultivated skilled generals and advisors.

Fun Fact: Formed the Oath of the Peach Garden with Guan Yu and Zhang Fei.

Liu Shan

Name: Liu Shan (劉禪, courtesy name Gongsi)

Years: 207–271 AD

Role: Second Emperor of Shu Han

Bio: Son of Liu Bei, he inherited the throne at a young age. His reign is often remembered as weak, ultimately leading to the surrender of Shu to the Jin dynasty.

Despite his reputation, he survived past Shu's fall and lived a long life under Jin rule.

Zhuge Liang

Name: Zhuge Liang (諸葛亮, courtesy name Kongming)

Years: 181–234 AD

Role: Chancellor and Strategist of Shu Han

Bio: Renowned strategist, inventor, and statesman. Served Liu Bei and then Liu Shan, managing both military campaigns and civil administration.

Famous for his Northern Expeditions against Wei and his wisdom in diplomacy and governance. Celebrated as the epitome of intelligence and loyalty in Chinese history.

Fun Fact: Zhuge Liang is credited with the invention of the repeating crossbow, steamed buns, and the Chinese lantern.

Fa Zheng

Name: Fa Zheng (法正, courtesy name Xiaozhi)

Years: 176–220 AD

Role: Strategist and Advisor

Bio: Close advisor to Liu Bei, known for intelligence and planning campaigns. Key in consolidating Shu Han’s early power.

Fei Yi

Name: Fei Yi (費禕, courtesy name Wenwei)

Bio: Successor to Jiang Wan as Shu’s leading statesman. Known for his diplomacy and calm leadership; assassinated unexpectedly during a festival.

Eastern Wu

Sun Jian

Name: Sun Jian (孫堅, courtesy name Wentai)

Years: 155–191 AD

Role: Founder and Warlord of Wu

Bio: Veteran general who rose during the collapse of the Han Dynasty.

Famous for his bravery and for recovering the Imperial Seal after the fall of Luoyang. Father of Sun Ce and Sun Quan, laying the foundations of the Wu Kingdom.

Sun Ce

Name: Sun Ce (孫策, courtesy name Bofu)

Years: 175–200 AD

Role: Warlord and Early Founder of Wu

Bio: Charismatic and daring leader who conquered much of the Jiangdong region.

Known as the “Little Conqueror,” his campaigns paved the way for the establishment of Eastern Wu under his brother Sun Quan. Killed young after consolidating the south.

Sun Quan

Name: Sun Quan (孫權, courtesy name Zhongmou)

Years: 182–252 AD

Role: Founding Emperor of Eastern Wu

Bio: Skilled ruler and diplomat who balanced warfare and alliance — notably against Cao Cao at the Battle of Red Cliffs (208 AD).

Reigned as Emperor of Wu for over 30 years, maintaining stability in southern China.

Sun He

Name: Sun He (孫和, courtesy name Ziyang)

Years: 224–253 AD

Role: Crown Prince of Eastern Wu

Bio: Son of Sun Quan, appointed crown prince.

Known for his competence but embroiled in court succession struggles, ultimately forced to step down and died in exile.

Sun Liang

Name: Sun Liang (孫亮, courtesy name Zixiao)

Years: 243–260 AD

Role: Second Emperor of Wu

Bio: Ascended the throne as a child. His reign was dominated by powerful regents such as Sun Jun and Sun Chen.

Deposed in a coup and later died in obscurity. His short rule reflected Wu’s growing political instability.

Sun Xiu

Name: Sun Xiu (孫休, courtesy name Zizhi)

Years: 235–264 AD

Role: Third Emperor of Wu

Bio: Succeeded his brother after a period of turmoil. A moderate and peace-minded ruler who reduced corruption but failed to restore Wu’s former power.

Remembered as virtuous but ineffective.

Sun Hao

Name: Sun Hao (孫皓, courtesy name Yuanzong)

Years: 243–284 AD

Role: Fourth and Last Emperor of Wu

Bio: Notorious for his cruelty and extravagance.

His misrule weakened Wu and alienated allies, leading to its conquest by the Jin Dynasty in 280 AD. Ended the era of the Three Kingdoms.

Bu Lianshi

Name: Bu Lianshi (步練師)

Years: ?–238 AD

Role: Consort of Sun Quan

Bio: Known for her intelligence, grace, and influence at court. Served as a stabilizing presence in Sun Quan’s later years.

Cheng Pu

Name: Cheng Pu (程普, courtesy name Demou)

Years: ?–215 AD

Role: Senior General of Wu

Bio: Served under Sun Jian, Sun Ce, and Sun Quan. A veteran of many early campaigns and a respected elder among Wu’s officers.

Ding Feng

Name: Ding Feng (丁奉, courtesy name Chengxian)

Years: ?–271 AD

Role: Wu General

Bio: Served from the reign of Sun Quan to Sun Hao. Distinguished himself at Yiling and in later defensive campaigns against Wei.

Gan Ning

Name: Gan Ning (甘寧, courtesy name Xingba)

Years: ?–215 AD

Role: Wu General

Bio: Former pirate who became a fierce warrior under Sun Quan. Known for daring raids and loyalty despite his rough beginnings.

Han Dang

Name: Han Dang (韓當, courtesy name Yigong)

Years: ?–227 AD

Role: Veteran General of Wu

Bio: Served the Sun family from Sun Jian through Sun Quan. Famed for his endurance and loyalty across multiple generations of rulers.

He Qi

Name: He Qi (賀齊, courtesy name Gongmiao)

Years: ?–227 AD

Role: General of Wu

Bio: Naval commander who helped Sun Quan consolidate control of the lower Yangtze. Famous for his flamboyant armor and disciplined troops.

Huang Gai

Name: Huang Gai (黃蓋, courtesy name Gongfu)

Years: ?–220 AD

Role: Veteran General of Wu

Bio: Loyal officer famed for his ruse at the Battle of Red Cliffs, where he feigned surrender to launch a devastating fire attack on Cao Cao’s fleet.

Lady Sun

Years: ?–? (early 3rd century)

Role: Sister of Sun Quan; Wife of Liu Bei

Bio: A bold and spirited woman reputed to have martial training. Her marriage to Liu Bei symbolized a temporary alliance between Wu and Shu.

Lady Wang

Name: Lady Wang (王氏)

Years: fl. 220s–250s AD

Role: Consort of Sun Quan

Bio: Mother of Sun He and Sun Ba. Played a significant role in Wu court politics during the succession struggles.

Known for her influence in favoring her son Sun He as crown prince and navigating court intrigues.

Lady Wu

Name: Lady Wu (吴国太)

Years: ?–202 AD

Role: Wife of Sun Jian, influential matriarch of Wu

Bio: Mother of Sun Quan and Sun Ce, she wielded considerable influence in Wu politics and succession matters.

Known for her guidance and counsel to her sons, shaping the stability and governance of the early Wu state. Posthumously honoured by Sun Quan as Empress Wulie.

Jin Dynasty

Sima Yi

Name: Sima Yi (司馬懿, courtesy name Zhongda)

Years: 179–251 AD

Role: Wei Regent and Founding Figure of the Jin Dynasty

Bio: Brilliant strategist and statesman who rose to power under Cao Rui.

Seized control of Wei through the Gaopingling Coup, establishing the Sima family's dominance that paved the way for Jin’s founding.

Sima Shi

Name: Sima Shi (司馬師, courtesy name Ziyuan)

Years: 208–255 AD

Role: Wei Regent

Bio: Eldest son of Sima Yi. Consolidated the family’s control over Wei after his father’s death.

A capable but stern ruler who suppressed multiple rebellions before dying from illness shortly after battle.

Sima Zhao

Name: Sima Zhao (司馬昭, courtesy name Zishang)

Years: 211–265 AD

Role: Wei Regent and Architect of the Jin Dynasty

Bio: Younger brother of Sima Shi and de facto ruler of Wei. Continued to centralize power, crushing Shu in 263 AD.

His ambition to usurp Wei was so open that it inspired the saying, “Everyone knows what Sima Zhao wants.”

Sima Yan

Name: Sima Yan (司馬炎, courtesy name Anshi)

Years: 236–290 AD

Role: Founding Emperor of the Jin Dynasty

Bio: Son of Sima Zhao. Officially ended the Cao Wei dynasty and founded the Jin in 266 AD as Emperor Wu.

Unified China in 280 AD after conquering Wu, marking the end of the Three Kingdoms period.

Deng Ai

Name: Deng Ai (鄧艾, courtesy name Shizai)

Years: 197–264 AD

Role: Wei General

Bio: Celebrated strategist who led the daring invasion through treacherous terrain to conquer Shu in 263 AD.

Later accused of treason by Zhong Hui and executed, despite his loyalty and success.

Du Yu

Name: Du Yu (杜預, courtesy name Yuankai)

Years: 222–284 AD

Role: General and scholar of Jin

Bio: Led the final campaign that destroyed Eastern Wu, unifying China under Jin. Also renowned as a scholar and commentator on the Zuo Zhuan.

Guo Hai

Name: Guo Huai (郭淮, courtesy name Boyu)

Years: ?–255 AD

Role: Wei General

Bio: Skilled commander who defended Wei’s western borders against Shu and Qiang forces.

Known for his composure, prudence, and ability to maintain stability in frontier regions.

Jia Chong

Name: Jia Chong (賈充, courtesy name Gongcheng)

Years: 217–282 AD

Role: Jin Statesman

Bio: Key political figure during the transition from Wei to Jin. Served Sima Zhao and Sima Yan, helping to establish Jin rule.

Notorious for his role in harsh political purges.

Wang Yuanji

Name: Wang Yuanji (王元姬)

Years: 217–268 AD

Role: Noblewoman of Wei and Jin

Bio: Wife of Sima Zhao and mother of Emperor Wu (Sima Yan). Renowned for her wisdom, virtue, and political insight.

Her guidance was instrumental in stabilizing the Sima household.

Wen Yang (Wen Chu)

Name: Wen Yang (文鴦, courtesy name Ciqian)

Years: 238–291 AD

Role: Jin General

Bio: Son of Wen Qin, famous for his unmatched strength and bravery. Known for his reckless courage and involvement in multiple uprisings during the Wei–Jin transition.

Xin Xianying

Name: Xin Xianying (辛憲英)

Years: 191–269 AD

Role: Wei Scholar and Stateswoman

Bio: Daughter of Xin Pi, known for her intellect and foresight.

Respected for offering wise political counsel during the turbulent Wei–Jin transition, one of few prominent women of her time.

Yang Hu

Name: Yang Hu (羊祜, courtesy name Shuzi)

Years: 221–278 AD

Role: General and statesman of Jin

Bio: Advocated peace and reconciliation with Wu while strengthening Jin’s borders. Respected by allies and enemies alike for his virtue and foresight.

Zhang Chunhua

Name: Zhang Chunhua (張春華)

Role: Matriarch of the Sima Clan

Bio: Wife of Sima Yi and mother of Sima Shi and Sima Zhao.

Remembered for her composure and intelligence, playing a quiet yet decisive role in securing the Sima family’s survival.

Zhong Hui

Name: Zhong Hui (鍾會, courtesy name Shiji)

Years: 225–264 AD

Role: Wei General and Scholar

Bio: Brilliant but ambitious commander who co-led the conquest of Shu.

Later rebelled against Wei in an attempt to seize power but was betrayed and killed by his own troops.

Other Figures

Zhang Jiao (Jue)

Name: Zhang Jiao (張角)

Years: ?–184 AD

Role: Rebel Leader

Bio: Leader of the Yellow Turban Rebellion; claimed to have supernatural powers and mobilized peasants in a large-scale uprising against the Han dynasty.

Died early in the rebellion, but his movement weakened central authority, setting the stage for the Three Kingdoms era.

Dong Zhuo

Name: Dong Zhuo (董卓, courtesy name Zhongying)

Years: ?–192 AD

Role: Warlord / Chancellor

Bio: Seized control of the imperial court, notorious for corruption and tyranny.

Assassinated by a coalition of warlords led by Wang Yun and Lü Bu.

Yuan Shao

Name: Yuan Shao (袁紹, courtesy name Benchu)

Years: 154–202 AD

Role: Warlord

Bio: Controlled northern China in the late Eastern Han period. Fought against Cao Cao at the Battle of Guandu (200 AD), but suffered a decisive defeat.

Yuan Shu

Name: Yuan Shu (袁術, courtesy name Gonglu)

Years: 155–199 AD

Role: Warlord

Bio: Controlled parts of central China; declared himself emperor, which alienated allies and contributed to his downfall.

Liu Biao

Name: Liu Biao (劉表, courtesy name Jingsheng)

Years: ?–208 AD

Role: Warlord

Bio: Governor of Jing Province; maintained neutrality during much of the warlord period, balancing between Cao Cao and Sun Quan.

Liu Zheng

Name: Liu Zheng (劉整, courtesy name Gongzhao)

Years: ?–?

Role: Warlord

Bio: Minor figure in the late Eastern Han period. Held regional power but had limited impact compared with the major warlords.

Gongsun Yuan

Name: Gongsun Yuan (公孫淵, courtesy name Wenyi)

Years: ? – 238 AD

Role: Warlord, self-proclaimed King of Yan

Bio: Grandson of Gongsun Du and son of Gongsun Kang, he ruled the Liaodong region and declared independence from Wei, styling himself King of Yan (燕王).

His brief reign is sometimes described as a “fourth kingdom” alongside Wei, Shu, and Wu. Defeated and executed by Sima Yi in coalition with the Xianbei and Goguryeo (modern Korean) in 238 AD after the Wei campaign into Liaodong.

Chen Gong

Name: Chen Gong (陳宮, courtesy name Gongtai)

Years: ?–198 AD

Role: Advisor / Strategist

Bio: Served Lü Bu as chief strategist; known for loyalty and cunning, ultimately executed after Lü Bu's defeat by Cao Cao.

Diao Chan

Name: Diaochan

Years: Legendary

Role: Famous Beauty

Bio: Mostly fictional figure from Romance of the Three Kingdoms; celebrated as one of the Four Beauties of ancient China.

Played a key role in stories involving Dong Zhuo and Lü Bu.

Fan Chou

Name: Fan Chou (樊稠)

Years: ?–195 AD

Role: General under Dong Zhuo

Bio: Rose to power after Dong Zhuo’s death and briefly became one of the dominant leaders of the Liang warlords.

Killed by Li Jue and Guo Si due to fear of his growing influence.

Gongsun Zan

Name: Gongsun Zan (公孫瓚, courtesy name Bogui)

Years: ?–199 AD

Role: Warlord / General

Bio: Northern warlord during the late Eastern Han period. Known for his strong cavalry forces and always riding a white horse. He was an early patron of Liu Bei.

Eventually defeated by rival Yuan Shao after years of conflict in northern China.

Guo Si (Guo Duo)

Name: Guo Si (郭汜)

Years: ?–197 AD

Role: General under Dong Zhuo

Bio: Collaborated with Li Jue to control the imperial court, but soon turned against him. Their civil war devastated Chang’an and symbolized the breakdown of Han authority.

Han Sui

Name: Han Sui (韓遂, courtesy name Wenyue)

Years: ?–215 AD

Role: Warlord

Bio: Allied with Ma Teng in northwest China; fought against Cao Cao during the late Eastern Han period.

He Jin

Name: He Jin (何進, courtesy name Suigao)

Years: ?–189 AD

Role: General-in-Chief of the Han

Bio: Brother of Empress He and uncle to Emperor Shao. His assassination by eunuchs triggered Dong Zhuo’s coup and the chaos that led to the Three Kingdoms era.

Hua Xiong

Name: Hua Xiong

Years: ?–? AD

Role: General

Bio: Historical general of the Eastern Han dynasty.

Many of his exploits, including his defeat by Guan Yu in the Romance of the Three Kingdoms, are likely fictionalized or dramatized.

Hu Zhen

Name: Hu Zhen (胡軫, courtesy name Wencai)

Years: ?–192 AD

Role: General under Dong Zhuo

Bio: Served Dong Zhuo and commanded forces during the anti-Dong coalition.

Defeated by Sun Jian and later killed during the fallout after Dong Zhuo’s assassination.

Huang Zu

Name: Huang Zu (黃祖, courtesy name Wenjing)

Years: d. 208 AD

Role: Warlord and general of Eastern Jing

Bio: Opposed Sun Jian and Sun Quan during the late Han period.

Best known for his defeat by Sun Quan at the Battle of Jiangxia, which secured Sun Quan’s control over the region.

Li Jue

Name: Li Jue (李傕, courtesy name Zhiran)

Years: ?–198 AD

Role: General under Dong Zhuo

Bio: After Dong Zhuo’s death, seized control of Chang’an with Guo Si. His infighting and cruelty deepened the chaos that followed the Han collapse.

Li Ru

Name: Li Ru (李儒, courtesy name Wenyou)

Years: fl. late 180s–190s AD

Role: Adviser to Dong Zhuo

Bio: Served as an influential advisor and strategist under the warlord Dong Zhuo.

Involved in political and military decisions during the late Eastern Han dynasty, known for cunning but morally questionable counsel.

Li Su

Name: Li Su (李肅)

Years: ?–192 AD

Role: General and conspirator

Bio: Played a key role in the assassination plot against Dong Zhuo, acting under Lü Bu’s support.

Though not a major warlord, his involvement helped topple one of the era’s most notorious tyrants.

Lü Bu

Name: Lü Bu (呂布, courtesy name Fengxian)

Years: ?–198 AD

Role: General / Warlord

Bio: Renowned warrior known for exceptional martial skill.

Served multiple warlords, including Ding Yuan and Dong Zhuo; executed after defeat by Cao Cao.

Ma Teng

Name: Ma Teng (馬騰, courtesy name Shoucheng)

Years: ?–212 AD

Role: Warlord

Bio: Controlled northwest China alongside Han Sui; father of Ma Chao; allied and clashed with various warlords during the late Eastern Han period.

Meng Huo

Name: Meng Huo

Years: ?–? AD

Role: Southern Chieftain

Bio: Leader of the Nanman in southern Shu; captured multiple times by Zhuge Liang in the northern campaigns of Shu.

Mentioned in near-contemporary sources, historians argue over his existence but most agree that the 'seven times freed' story is fictional.

Wang Yun

Name: Wang Yun (王允, courtesy name Zishi)

Years: ?–192 AD

Role: Minister of the Han

Bio: High-ranking official who conspired with Lü Bu and Diao Chan to overthrow Dong Zhuo. His brief success ended with his execution after Dong Zhuo’s faction retaliated.

Xu Rong

Name: Xu Rong (徐榮)

Years: ?–192 AD

Role: General under Dong Zhuo

Bio: Noted for defeating Sun Jian at Yangren during the coalition against Dong Zhuo.

Killed later in battle during internal conflicts after Dong Zhuo’s death.

Zhang Ji

Name: Zhang Ji (張濟)

Years: ?–196 AD

Role: General under Dong Zhuo

Bio: Served Dong Zhuo and later joined Li Jue and Guo Si after Dong’s death.

Controlled parts of the central plains but was killed during a campaign in Nanyang. Uncle of Zhang Xiu.

Zuo Ci

Name: Zuo Ci (左慈, courtesy name Yuanfang)

Years: ?–? AD

Role: Taoist Mystic

Bio: Taoist practitioner and mystic; performed legendary feats and trickery.

Appears in Romance of the Three Kingdoms as a figure of magical prowess; historical existence is partially verified.

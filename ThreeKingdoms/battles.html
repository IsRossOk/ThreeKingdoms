Zhang Jiao (leader), Zhang Bao, Zhang Liang

Han Imperial Forces

He Jin (general), local militia leaders

Outcome / Significance: Rebellion suppressed, weakened Han central government, rise of local warlords, set stage for Three Kingdoms.

Late 180s AD Dong Zhuo's Rise and Rule

Factions: Dong Zhuo’s forces vs. Coalition of warlords opposing him

Dong Zhuo’s Side

Dong Zhuo (warlord), Lü Bu (general/adopted son), Li Ru (minister)

Opposition / Coalition

Wang Yun (court official), Yuan Shao, Yuan Shu, Sun Jian, other warlords

Location: Luoyang and surrounding central China

Background: Seized power after Emperor Ling’s death; notorious for corruption and cruelty; dominated the imperial court.

Outcome / Significance: Assassinated in 192 AD by Lü Bu and Wang Yun; highlighted Han weakness; sparked further warlord conflicts; set stage for Three Kingdoms.

Extra Notes: Dramatised heavily in Romance of the Three Kingdoms; historically pivotal as the early power struggle following the death of Emperor Ling.

200 AD Battle of Guandu

Factions / Coalitions: Cao Cao’s forces vs. Yuan Shao’s northern army

Cao Cao

Cao Cao (commander), Xiahou Dun, Xiahou Yuan, Guo Jia

Yuan Shao

Yuan Shao (commander), Yuan Tan, Tian Feng, Shen Pei

Location: Guandu (near modern-day Kaifeng, Henan)

Background / Cause: Yuan Shao, controlling northern China, held a massive army but suffered from poor logistics and internal discord. Cao Cao, with a smaller and disciplined force, sought to stop Yuan Shao’s expansion southward.

Tactics / Strategy: Cao Cao fortified his position at Guandu, using the terrain strategically. The pivotal moment came when Cao Cao raided and burned Yuan Shao’s supply depot at Wuchao, crippling the northern army’s resources and morale.

Outcome / Significance: Decisive victory for Cao Cao; consolidated power in northern China and weakened Yuan Shao’s influence. This battle is remembered as a classic example of strategy and discipline overcoming numerical superiority.

Extra Notes: Guo Jia’s strategic advice was crucial in Cao Cao’s planning. The battle is dramatized in Romance of the Three Kingdoms, highlighting Cao Cao’s clever tactics and the internal weaknesses of Yuan Shao’s command. It set the stage for Cao Cao’s eventual dominance in the central plains and the foundation of the Wei state.

200 AD Death of Sun Ce & Succession to Sun Quan

Factions / Coalitions: Sun Ce’s forces consolidating Jiangdong

Sun Ce / Sun Quan

Sun Ce (deceased), Sun Quan (successor), Zhou Yu, Lü Fan

Location: Jiangdong region (modern Jiangsu, Zhejiang)

Background: Sun Ce, known as the “Little Conqueror,” expanded his influence in the south through decisive campaigns. His untimely death from illness or assassination led to Sun Quan taking command.

Outcome / Significance: Smooth succession allowed Sun Quan to consolidate Eastern Wu, maintaining stability in the south and preparing for future conflicts with Cao Cao and Liu Bei.

203–208 AD Faction Consolidations and Campaigns

Overview: Following the deaths of Yuan Shao and Sun Ce, and Liu Bei’s initial victories at Bowang, the three main factions consolidate their territories and strengthen their positions for the coming years of regional conflict.

Cao Cao – Northern Control

Factions / Figures: Cao Cao, Xiahou Dun, Xiahou Yuan, Guo Jia

Details: Secures northern China, fortifies supply lines, and exerts authority over former Yuan Shao territories. Prepares southern campaigns while stabilizing the north.

Outcome: Consolidation of northern territories, control over key strategic cities, and increased readiness for southern operations.

Liu Bei – Jing Province Expansion

Factions / Figures: Liu Bei, Zhuge Liang, Guan Yu, Zhang Fei

Details: Strengthens holdings in Jing Province, including Xiangyang and Jiangling. Implements Zhuge Liang’s guidance for governance and military organization.

Outcome: Secured central China foothold, enhanced troop loyalty, and improved administrative structure for future campaigns.

Sun Quan – Jiangdong Consolidation

Factions / Figures: Sun Quan, Zhou Yu, Lu Su, Gan Ning

Details: Expands southern territories, fortifies river defenses, and organizes regional forces. Strengthens alliances within the south and readies forces for potential northern incursions.

Outcome: Stabilized southern provinces, fortified positions along key rivers, and prepared the faction for both defense and future expansion.

Significance: These movements establish the early power structures of the Three Kingdoms, and their early territorial control, setting the scene for the Three Kingdoms era to begin.

208 AD Battle of Changban

Factions / Coalitions: Cao Cao’s forces pursuing Liu Bei; Liu Bei’s retreating army

Cao Cao

Cao Cao (commander), Xiahou Dun, Xiahou Yuan

Details: Pursues Liu Bei’s forces aggressively after securing northern China. Aims to eliminate Liu Bei before he can consolidate his holdings in Jing Province.

Outcome: Inflicts heavy casualties on Liu Bei’s army but fails to capture Liu Bei himself; Liu Bei retreats southward, preserving core forces for future engagements.

Liu Bei

Liu Bei (commander), Zhao Yun, Zhang Fei, Guan Yu

Details: Executes a rapid retreat south while protecting civilians and key personnel. Zhao Yun famously rescues Liu Bei’s infant son during the chaos.

Outcome: Preserves the leadership and core troops; maintains a foothold in Jing Province and sets the stage for strategic alliances with Sun Quan in the south.

Significance: Demonstrates Liu Bei’s strategic withdrawal under pressure, showcases heroism of key generals, and directly precedes the formation of the southern coalition leading into the Red Cliffs campaign.

208 AD Battle of Red Cliffs

Cao Cao / Wei

Cao Cao (commander), Xu Huang, Zhang Liao, Zhou Yu (advisors)

Sun Quan / Wu

Sun Quan (commander), Zhou Yu (strategist), Lu Su, Huang Gai

Liu Bei / Shu Han

Liu Bei (commander), Zhuge Liang (advisor), Guan Yu, Zhang Fei

Location: Yangtze River near modern-day Chibi, Hubei

Background: Cao Cao, controlling northern China, attempts to conquer the south and unify the empire. Sun Quan and Liu Bei form an alliance to resist him. Zhuge Liang advises Liu Bei, strategists like Zhou Yu plan key defensive and offensive maneuvers.

Outcome / Significance: Decisive victory for the Sun Quan-Liu Bei alliance. Cao Cao retreats north, establishing the de facto division between northern Wei and southern Wu/Shu territories. Solidifies Zhuge Liang’s reputation as a strategist; sets the stage for future Shu-Wu cooperation.

Extra Notes: Considered one of the most famous battles in Chinese history; combination of naval tactics, fire attacks, and clever strategy. Often dramatized in Romance of the Three Kingdoms but based on historical events.

210s AD Cao Wei – Consolidation & Northern Campaigns

Cao Cao / Wei

Cao Cao (commander and King of Wei), Xiahou Dun, Xiahou Yuan, Guo Jia

Location: Northern and central China

Background: After Red Cliffs, Cao Cao retreats north but consolidates his power over northern China. Focuses on stabilizing territories, suppressing local uprisings, and strengthening his military and administrative control.

Outcome / Significance: Cao Wei maintains dominance in the north; lays foundations for future northern campaigns. Cao Cao’s strategic positioning ensures that Shu Han and Wu remain limited to southern and southwestern regions.

Extra Notes: Cao Cao is titled King of Wei during this period, formally consolidating his authority before Cao Pi later becomes emperor in 220 AD.

210s AD Liu Bei – Consolidation of Yi Province

Liu Bei / Shu Han

Liu Bei (commander and future Emperor), Zhuge Liang (strategist), Guan Yu, Zhang Fei, Huang Zhong

Location: Yi Province (modern Sichuan)

Background: After Red Cliffs, Liu Bei secures Yi Province to establish a stable base for Shu Han. Key early battles include Mount Dingjun, where Huang Zhong achieves a decisive victory over local rival forces, helping Liu Bei consolidate his authority in the region.

Outcome / Significance: Establishes Shu Han’s territorial base; strengthens Liu Bei’s control over southwestern China; sets the stage for future northern campaigns against Cao Wei.

Extra Notes: Zhuge Liang’s strategic advice is crucial in securing alliances and governing newly conquered territories; Mount Dingjun victory boosts morale and prestige for Shu Han forces.

214-215 AD Battle of Xiaoyao Ford

Cao Wei

Zhang Liao (commander), Li Dian, Yue Jin - operating under Cao Cao's authority

Eastern Wu / Sun Quan

Sun Quan (commander), Ling Tong, Gan Ning, Lü Meng

Location: Hefei, Huainan Province (modern Anhui)

Background: Following the Red Cliffs defeat, Cao Cao retained control of Hefei as a strategic northern stronghold against Eastern Wu. In 214 AD, Sun Quan personally led a major offensive to seize it. Zhang Liao, commanding a vastly smaller Wei garrison, prepared a bold pre-emptive strike.

Outcome / Significance: Zhang Liao led a surprise assault at dawn, scattering Wu’s forward forces and nearly capturing Sun Quan himself. Despite being outnumbered, the Wei defenders held the fortress. Sun Quan was forced to retreat, preserving Hefei as a critical Wei stronghold for decades. The victory cemented Zhang Liao’s fame as one of Cao Cao’s greatest generals.

Extra Notes: The battle is legendary for Zhang Liao’s valor and tactical brilliance. His small cavalry charge is considered one of the most daring actions of the era. The engagement also stabilized the border between Wei and Wu temporarily, establishing a long-term front along the Huai River region.

210s AD Guan Yu vs Sun Quan / Lü Meng

Shu Han / Guan Yu

Guan Yu (commander), Liu Bei (patron), Zhang Fei (support)

Eastern Wu / Sun Quan

Lü Meng (commander), Sun Quan (ruler), Gan Ning (lieutenant)

Location: Jing Province (modern Hubei / Hunan)

Background: After the Red Cliffs alliance, Guan Yu is tasked with defending and expanding Shu control in Jing Province. He initially achieves successes against local forces loyal to Wu. However, Lü Meng launches a cunning counterattack, taking Guan Yu by surprise.

Outcome / Significance: Guan Yu is captured and executed in 219 AD; Eastern Wu regains control of Jing Province. Marks a significant setback for Shu Han’s northern strategy and alters the balance of power between the Three Kingdoms.

Extra Notes: Lü Meng’s use of deception and strategy in this campaign is highly regarded; Guan Yu’s death becomes a legendary event, dramatized heavily in Romance of the Three Kingdoms, reinforcing his image as a paragon of loyalty and martial skill.

220 AD Death of Cao Cao / Cao Pi Becomes Emperor of Wei

Cao Wei

Cao Cao (deceased), Cao Pi (successor), Xiahou Dun, Xiahou Yuan

Other Key Figures

Liu Bei (Shu Han), Sun Quan (Wu)

Location: Luoyang and northern China

Background: Cao Cao, having dominated northern China for decades, dies. His son Cao Pi formally ends the Han dynasty by compelling Emperor Xian to abdicate, establishing the state of Cao Wei and officially beginning the Three Kingdoms period.

Outcome / Significance: Cao Pi becomes the first Emperor of Wei, consolidating northern China under his rule. The formal division of China into three kingdoms – Wei, Shu, and Wu – is recognized. Marks a major political shift and sets the stage for subsequent power struggles and military campaigns among the Three Kingdoms.

Extra Notes: Cao Cao’s policies and military campaigns laid the foundation for Cao Wei’s power. The abdication of Emperor Xian symbolizes the effective end of Han authority, though Shu and Wu continue to claim legitimacy as Han successors.

221–223 AD Liu Bei Proclaimed Emperor of Shu Han / Battle of Xiaoting (Yiling)

Shu Han

Liu Bei (Emperor), Zhuge Liang (Chancellor and strategist), Guan Yu (before Jing province events), Zhao Yun

Eastern Wu

Sun Quan (leader), Lu Xun (general), Zhou Tai

Location: Southern China, primarily around Yiling (modern Hubei)

Background: After proclaiming himself Emperor of Shu Han, Liu Bei launched a campaign to avenge his sworn brother Guan Yu’s death and reclaim Jing province from Sun Quan’s control. Strategic miscalculations and strong Wu defenses led to Shu’s defeat.

Outcome / Significance: Liu Bei’s forces suffer a decisive defeat; marks the end of his southern ambitions. Liu Bei dies in 223 AD shortly afterward, leaving Zhuge Liang as regent to guide Shu Han. Wu consolidates control over Jing province, shaping the territorial lines of the Three Kingdoms.

Extra Notes: Battle of Xiaoting/Yiling is famous for Lu Xun’s strategic use of fire attacks. The defeat heavily influences Zhuge Liang’s later northern expeditions against Wei rather than southern campaigns.

225 AD Zhuge Liang’s Southern Campaign

Factions / Coalitions: Shu Han forces under Zhuge Liang vs. Nanman tribes; minor Wu influence

Shu Han

Zhuge Liang (commander), Shu generals

Opposition

Nanman tribal leaders, including Meng Huo (semi-legendary), minor Wu-affiliated forces

Location: Southern borders of Shu Han (modern-day Yunnan, Guizhou, and parts of Sichuan)

Background: Following the consolidation of Shu Han’s core territories, Zhuge Liang led a southern campaign to pacify the Nanman tribes, secure trade routes, and prevent unrest in Shu’s rear. Meng Huo, a semi-legendary local leader, is traditionally depicted as a key figure resisting Shu’s authority.

Outcome / Significance: Successfully subjugated the Nanman tribes; reinforced Shu Han’s southern frontier and allowed focus on northern campaigns against Wei.

Extra Notes: Distinguished by a combination of diplomacy and selective military action; Meng Huo’s legendary “Seven Captures” story is often highlighted in Romance of the Three Kingdoms but may be more folklore than fact.

228–234 AD Zhuge Liang’s Northern Expeditions

Factions / Coalitions: Shu Han under Zhuge Liang vs. Cao Wei forces

Shu Han

Zhuge Liang (chancellor and strategist), Wei Yan, Jiang Wei

Cao Wei

Cao Rui (Emperor), Sima Yi, Guo Huai

Location: Northern China, along strategic border passes and Wei territories

Background: After Liu Bei’s death, Zhuge Liang led a series of military campaigns to secure Shu Han’s northern border and attempt to weaken Wei’s hold in the Central Plains. Campaigns were carefully planned but limited by logistical constraints.

Outcome / Significance: Multiple campaigns failed to decisively defeat Wei. Strengthened Shu’s military organization, enhanced Zhuge Liang’s reputation as a brilliant strategist, but ultimately Shu could not expand its territory significantly.

Extra Notes: Included notable campaigns such as the Battle of Jieting and multiple northern expeditions. Showcases the tension between strategic planning and logistical limitations during the Three Kingdoms period.

222–229 AD Sun Quan Declares Independence / Becomes Emperor of Wu

Factions / Coalitions: Eastern Wu vs. Cao Wei and Shu Han (intermittent tensions)

Eastern Wu

Sun Quan (leader and later emperor), Zhou Yu, Lu Xun

Rival Factions

Cao Wei forces (various generals), Shu Han occasional incursions

Location: Jiangdong / Yangtze River delta region

Background: Following Sun Ce’s death, Sun Quan consolidated power in the south, stabilizing Jiangdong. He navigated a delicate balance of diplomacy and military action to maintain independence from both Wei and Shu while expanding territorial control.

Outcome / Significance: Sun Quan firmly establishes Eastern Wu as a sovereign power, setting the stage for the Three Kingdoms period. Wu remains a major southern power for decades.

Extra Notes: Sun Quan’s political skill in managing internal factions and external threats ensured Wu’s survival. Key generals like Zhou Yu and Lu Xun played important roles in defending and expanding Wu’s territories.

234 AD Battle of Wuzhang Plains

Factions / Coalitions: Shu Han (Zhuge Liang) vs. Cao Wei forces

Shu Han

Zhuge Liang (chancellor and strategist), Jiang Wei (general), Ma Su (advisor)

Cao Wei

Sima Yi (general), Zhang He, Guo Huai

Location: Wuzhang Plains (near present-day Shaanxi province)

Background: Zhuge Liang launched his final northern expedition to pressure Wei and expand Shu influence. The campaign involved strategic standoffs, fortifications, and supply challenges rather than large-scale pitched battles.

Outcome / Significance: Zhuge Liang dies of illness during the campaign. Shu Han retreats, ending the era of aggressive northern campaigns. This marks the loss of Shu’s most capable strategist and a turning point in the power balance among the Three Kingdoms.

Extra Notes: Zhuge Liang’s death is often dramatized as the final straw in the decline of Shu’s military ambitions. Sima Yi’s cautious tactics prevented a Shu breakthrough, preserving Wei’s northern territories.

234 AD Battle of Hefei

Factions / Coalitions: Cao Wei vs. Eastern Wu

Cao Wei

Sima Yi (commander), Zhang He, Cao Shuang

Eastern Wu

Sun Quan (commander), Lu Xun (general), Zhou Tai (general)

Location: Hefei (present-day Anhui province)

Background: Wu forces launched an offensive to capture Hefei and threaten Wei’s southern territories. Sima Yi prepared strong fortifications and successfully defended the city.

Outcome / Significance: Cao Wei successfully repels Wu; demonstrates Wei’s defensive strength and Sima Yi’s strategic skill. Wu fails to expand northwards at this time.

Extra Notes: Hefei remained a key defensive stronghold for Wei. Multiple attempts by Wu to take it were repelled, solidifying Wei’s southern border.

238 AD Sima Yi’s Liaodong Campaign

Factions / Coalitions: Cao Wei vs. Gongsun family (Liaodong)

Cao Wei

Sima Yi (commander), Wei generals

Liaodong

Gongsun Yuan (warlord), local forces

247–262 AD Jiang Wei’s Northern Expeditions

Factions / Coalitions: Shu Han under Jiang Wei vs. Cao Wei forces

Shu Han

Jiang Wei (commander), Shu generals

Cao Wei

Sima Yi (early campaigns), Sima Shi, Sima Zhao, Wei generals

Location: Northern frontier of Shu Han, bordering Wei territories

Background: Following Zhuge Liang’s death, Jiang Wei continued the policy of Northern Expeditions to pressure Wei, attempting to restore Shu Han’s dominance and avenge earlier losses.

Outcome / Significance: Repeated campaigns failed to achieve decisive gains; drained Shu’s resources and manpower; highlighted the growing strength and stability of Wei under the Sima clan.

Extra Notes: Jiang Wei’s persistence is remembered as both heroic and tragic; these expeditions set the stage for Shu’s eventual decline and the later conquest by Wei/Jin forces.

Mid-240s–260s AD Decline of Eastern Wu

Factions / Coalitions: Eastern Wu under successive emperors; internal factions and rivalries weaken the state

Wu Leadership

Sun Quan (d. 252 AD), Sun Liang, Sun Xiu, Sun Hao

Others

Shu and Wei are less directly involved; internal rebellions and administrative corruption dominate the decline

Location: Jiangdong / Yangtze River delta region

Background: After the strong leadership of Sun Quan, successive rulers struggled with internal corruption, weak central authority, and repeated succession crises. Wu remained militarily capable but politically fragmented, making it increasingly vulnerable to external pressure.

Outcome / Significance: Wu’s weakening set the stage for the Jin conquest; the state survived several decades beyond Shu’s fall but was steadily declining in cohesion and military power.

Extra Notes: Despite the decline, Wu maintained cultural and economic strength in its heartlands, and its legacy influenced the later Eastern Jin dynasty.

Mid-240s–260s AD Cao Rui’s Death & Rise of Sima Influence

Factions / Coalitions: Cao Wei leadership; Sima family consolidating military and political control

Cao Wei

Cao Rui (emperor, d. 239 AD), successors under weak emperors

Sima Clan

Sima Yi (strategist, regent), Sima Zhao (son, rising power)

Location: Northern China – Wei territories

Background: Following Cao Rui’s death, the Wei emperors were relatively weak. Sima Yi, a seasoned general and strategist, along with his son Sima Zhao, began consolidating power behind the throne, controlling both military and administrative affairs.

Outcome / Significance: The Sima family effectively became the real rulers of Wei, paving the way for the eventual establishment of the Jin dynasty under Sima Yan. This period marks the political weakening of the Cao family while the Sima clan’s influence solidifies.

Extra Notes: Sima Yi had already demonstrated political acumen during earlier campaigns and northern expeditions. Sima Zhao’s steady rise ensured continuity of Sima dominance, setting the stage for the eventual unification under Jin.

266 AD Sima Zhao’s Death & Sima Yan Becomes Emperor of Jin

Factions / Coalitions: Wei imperial court; Sima family consolidation

Cao Wei

Emperor Cao Fang (nominal), remnants of the Wei royal house

Sima Clan

Sima Zhao (d. 266 AD), Sima Yan (son, becomes first Emperor of Jin)

Location: Northern China – Wei territories transitioning to Jin

Background: Sima Zhao had consolidated power over Wei’s military and court. Upon his death, his son Sima Yan inherited this authority and formally ended the Cao Wei dynasty by declaring himself Emperor of the Jin dynasty.

Outcome / Significance: Official end of Cao Wei; beginning of the Jin dynasty; Sima family fully in control, setting the stage for reunification of China.

Extra Notes: Though the transition was relatively smooth due to the Sima family’s prior dominance, it marked a significant shift in China’s political landscape and the final step toward the eventual conquest of Wu.

271 AD Death of Liu Shan – Philosophical End of Shu Han

Factions / Coalitions: Shu Han remnants

Shu Han

Liu Shan (last Emperor), Zhuge Liang (historical influence, deceased)

Location: Chengdu, Shu Han heartland

Background: After decades of internal governance and military campaigns, Shu Han had already lost most of its military strength. Liu Shan’s death symbolically marked the end of the state.

Outcome / Significance: While Shu Han had technically been weakened and controlled by local forces, Liu Shan’s death represented the philosophical and symbolic end of the Shu dynasty.

Extra Notes: Some historians consider this the final closure of Shu Han’s story in the Three Kingdoms period, though the territories were not yet formally absorbed into Jin.

280 AD Conquest of Wu – Jin Unifies China

Factions / Coalitions: Jin dynasty vs Eastern Wu

Jin Dynasty

Sima Yan (Emperor), Wang Jun, Du Yu

Eastern Wu

Sun Hao (last Emperor), Wu generals and loyalists

Location: Yangtze River basin, Eastern China

Background: After decades of relative stability in Jin, Sima Yan launched a large-scale campaign to conquer Wu, the last remaining independent Three Kingdoms state.

Outcome / Significance: Jin successfully annexed Wu, officially ending the Three Kingdoms period and reuniting China under a single dynasty.

Extra Notes: This marked the consolidation of power under the Sima family and the beginning of the Western Jin dynasty era.
